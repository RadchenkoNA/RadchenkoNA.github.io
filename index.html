What does Russia’s war in Ukraine tell us about Russia’s domestic political regime and its ability to project power abroad?
One million casualties (Pancevski 2024) and one botched coup attempt later, Russia finds itself mired in a self-destructive war of attrition with Ukraine, which I argue, though significantly undermining its ability to project power abroad, has dealt little damage to its domestic political regime. In particular, I will be focusing on the effects of Russia’s personnel and equipment losses in Ukraine and international sanctions on its hard power capabilities, and the impact of the war on the support the regime enjoys domestically. Firstly, Russia’s failed venture into Ukraine has definitively revealed its military to be far less of a “force to be reckoned with” than had previously been presumed. Having started the war with professional soldiers, Russia has cycled through volunteers, Wagner mercenaries, convicts, conscripts, foreign fighters and most recently, turned to manpower supplied by North Korea (Copp et al 2024). As supplies run thin, fresh recruits and conscripts are increasingly facing the ‘meat grinder’ in airsoft-made flak jackets (Hambling 2022) and with Soviet, in some cases, even pre-Soviet hardware (Cook 2024). For the more than 70,000 consequent personnel losses in Ukraine (Ivshina 2024) Russia’s territorial gains have been relatively-speaking limited. As of the 16th of October 2024, Russia occupies only 18% of Ukraine’s territory (CFR 2024), up from the 7.1%, held by separatists in the Donbass and Russia (Crimea), at the end of 2021 (CIA 2021). What Russia had hoped would be a 10-day blitzkrieg in Ukraine (Zabrodskyi et al 2022) has turned into a catch-22 from which both sides have sought to disengage, albeit unsuccessfully (Charap, Radchenko 2024). At this rate, even if Russia should manage to outlast Ukraine on the battlefield, the victory secured would be pyrrhic and more evoke sighs of relief from Moscow rather than ecstatic celebration. Secondly, though Western-imposed sanctions on Russia have failed to cripple its economy, whose GDP the IMF forecast to grow by 3.6% this year (IMF 2024), they have served to limit the reach of Russia’s influence abroad. Indeed, given Russia’s propensity to coercive diplomacy vis-a-vis its gas reserves, the EU has made a conscious effort to wean itself off of Russian gas with imports falling from 45% in 2021 to just 18% in 2024 (Borell 2024). The cessation of operations of more than 1000 companies in Russia since February 2022 (CELI 2024) has also hampered its potential for coercive diplomacy in the form of the seizure of foreign company assets; something it has relied on in the past (Braw 2023). Today, the Russian courts fined Google 2 Undecillion Roubles, “a figure that dwarfs the size of the global economy” (Hanna, Chernova 2024), but with the partial withdrawal of Google from the Russian markets and the bankruptcy of Google’s Russia Division, such action can only be symbolic. Russia is a country that has historically relied on hostage diplomacy, but with declining interdependence between the West and Russia, this tried-and-tested strategy will likely be rendered obsolete. Finally, though there has been some vocal opposition to Russia’s war in Ukraine, much of Russia is either indifferent to or supportive of the regime. While it is reasonable to question the legitimacy of the Presidential elections held this March, the fact remains that Putin won his historic 5th term with 88% of the vote (Gos. Duma 2024). Comparing this with his approval ratings in the polls, which rose from 71% in February 2022 to reach a peak of 87% between June and July of 2024 (Levada 2024), makes his victory margin start to look less fantastical. Of course when the only other viable candidate is a kowtowing sycophant, it would be unfair to say that the electorate was particularly spoiled for choice. In conclusion, Russia’s involvement in Ukraine can only be viewed as an agonizing foreign policy blunder that has seen it relegated to secondary power status on the world stage, both militarily and economically, but failed to seriously dampen domestic support for the regime. Over the past two decades, but especially over these past few years, Russia has gone from acting as China’s “big brother”, to being its overzealous “little brother”. Indeed, for many, the decline of Russia as a world power was a foregone conclusion even before the war. To quote Dylan Thomas:“Though wise men at their end know dark is right … They do not go gentle into that good night”. In this sense, perhaps Russia’s ill-conceived Ukraine gambit was but a final, desperate act of cathartic rebellion against the bitter inexorability of its decline. Word Count: 748
